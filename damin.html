What is a scion? A scion is a woody cutting taken directly from a named, producing mother tree. Each scion from Estes Avocado Company is 6–8 inches long with a minimum of 3 nodes (bud sites). You graft it onto rootstock to grow a true-to-variety tree. Grafted trees fruit in 1–3 years vs. 8–20 years for seed-grown trees with unpredictable fruit quality. All scions are cut after your order is received — never pre-cut and stored. This ensures maximum viability and freshness.

What about seeds? We also sell fresh avocado seeds (pits) — 10 seeds for $40. Seeds are useful for growing your own rootstock to graft onto, or for experimental breeding. Note: seed-grown trees do not reproduce the parent variety's fruit characteristics — for true-to-variety trees, always graft a scion onto your seedling rootstock.

Why scions — not trees?

Grafted trees are excellent, but they're expensive to ship and limit your rootstock choices. Scionwood is compact, ships well when properly packaged, and puts you in control of which rootstock your tree grows on — which matters for soil type, cold tolerance, and disease resistance.

If you have a seedling rootstock, or you're working with a local nursery on custom grafts, fresh scionwood from a known mother tree is exactly what you need.

Grafting avocados — an overview

Avocados are grafted because seed-grown trees take 8–20 years to produce and yield genetically unpredictable fruit. Grafted trees reproduce the exact variety and typically fruit in 1–3 years.

What you need

  • A healthy rootstock seedling — 6–18 months old, pencil-diameter stem
  • A fresh scion with 3+ nodes (what we sell)
  • A sharp, sterilized grafting knife or single-edge razor blade
  • Grafting tape or parafilm to seal the union
  • Optional: grafting wax or sealant for added protection

Common graft types

Cleft graft — Most beginner-friendly. Cut the rootstock flat and split it; shape the scion into a wedge and press into the split. Works well when rootstock is thicker than the scion.

Side-veneer graft — Used by most professional nurseries. Long slanting cuts on both pieces create maximum cambium contact. Great for similar-diameter stems.

Whip-and-tongue — Best when rootstock and scion are matched in diameter. Interlocking tongues create a self-aligning, strong union.

Rootstock for Placer County

For our climate, Zutano and Duke seedlings are the most commonly used cold-hardy rootstocks. For heavy clay or poor drainage, Duke 7 or Dusa clonal rootstocks offer better disease resistance. Local Placer County pickup customers can discuss rootstock sourcing with Damin directly.

Rooting pomegranate cuttings

Pomegranate cuttings root easily without grafting. Take your dormant cutting, let the cut end callous for 24 hours, then plant upright in a well-draining mix with 2–3 nodes below the surface. Keep moist but not wet. Roots typically develop in 4–8 weeks in warm conditions. No grafting required.
